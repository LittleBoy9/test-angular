He was the first Asian to win the Nobel Prize for Literature in 1913 for his collection of poems, Gitanjali. He was an advocate of Indian nationalism and contributed significantly to the Indian independence movement through his writings and political activism. His works continue to be celebrated for their lyrical beauty, philosophical depth, and social commentary. Rabindranath Tagore FRAS was a Bengali polymath who worked as a poet, writer, playwright, composer, philosopher, social reformer and painter.[4][5][6] He reshaped Bengali literature and music as well as Indian art with Contextual Modernism in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Author of the "profoundly sensitive, fresh and beautiful" poetry of Gitanjali,[7] he became in 1913 the first non-European and the first lyricist to win the Nobel Prize in Literature.[8] Tagore's poetic songs were viewed as spiritual and mercurial; however, his "elegant prose and magical poetry" remain largely unknown outside Bengal.[9] He was a fellow of the Royal Asiatic Society. Referred to as "the Bard of Bengal",[10][5][6] Tagore was known by sobriquets: Gurudeb, Kobiguru, Biswokobi.[a] A Bengali Brahmin from Calcutta with ancestral gentry roots in Burdwan district[12] and Jessore, Tagore wrote poetry as an eight-year-old.[13] At the age of sixteen, he released his first substantial poems under the pseudonym Bhānusiṃha ("Sun Lion"), which were seized upon by literary authorities as long-lost classics.[14] By 1877 he graduated to his first short stories and dramas, published under his real name. As a humanist, universalist, internationalist, and ardent critic of nationalism,[15] he denounced the British Raj and advocated independence from Britain. As an exponent of the Bengal Renaissance, he advanced a vast canon that comprised paintings, sketches and doodles, hundreds of texts, and some two thousand songs; his legacy also endures in his founding of Visva-Bharati University.[16][17] Tagore modernised Bengali art by spurning rigid classical forms and resisting linguistic strictures. His novels, stories, songs, dance-dramas, and essays spoke to topics political and personal. Gitanjali (Song Offerings), Gora (Fair-Faced) and Ghare-Baire (The Home and the World) are his best-known works, and his verse, short stories, and novels were acclaimed—or panned—for their lyricism, colloquialism, naturalism, and unnatural contemplation. His compositions were chosen by two nations as national anthems: India's "Jana Gana Mana" and Bangladesh's "Amar Shonar Bangla". The Sri Lankan national anthem was inspired by his work.[18] The Tagore family was at the forefront of the Bengal renaissance. They hosted the publication of literary magazines; theatre and recitals of Bengali and Western classical music featured there regularly. He was born on May 7, 1861, in Kolkata, India, and was the youngest son of Debendranath Tagore, a prominent figure in the Brahmo Samaj, a religious and social reform movement in 19th century Bengal. Tagore received his education at home, where he was exposed to a wide range of subjects including literature, music, and art. Tagore is considered to be one of the most significant literary figures of the 20th century, and his contributions to Bengali and Indian literature are immense. He was the first non-European to win the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1913 for his collection of poems, Gitanjali (Song Offerings). The poems in Gitanjali are deeply spiritual and reflect Tagore's belief in the unity of all religions. Tagore was also a prolific writer of novels, short stories, and plays. Some of his most famous works include The Home and the World, Ghare-Baire, and Chokher Bali. His plays, such as The Post Office and The King of the Dark Chamber, are still performed in India today. Tagore's works often dealt with themes of social and political reform, and he was a strong advocate for Indian independence. In addition to his literary contributions, Tagore was also a talented musician and painter. He composed over 2,000 songs, which are known as Rabindra Sangeet, and are still popular in India and Bangladesh. Tagore's paintings, which he began creating in his later years, were also highly regarded. Tagore's legacy continues to influence Bengali and Indian culture to this day. His works have been translated into numerous languages, and his ideas on education and social reform are still studied and debated. Tagore's belief in the power of art and literature to promote understanding and unity between different cultures and religions continues to inspire people around the world.
